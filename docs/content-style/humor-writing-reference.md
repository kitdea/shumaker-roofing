# Humor Writing Reference

Source material condensed from two articles: "Writing Humor: The Art of Making Readers Laugh" (Mark Bowden, HubSpot/Creative Resource, Aug 2023) and "7 Simple Tricks to Add Humor to Your Writing (without offending anyone)" (Gay Merrill, Medium, Feb 2017).

This is a **style reference**, not a mandate — humor is optional seasoning for blog content, not a required element. Use judgment on whether a given post benefits from it (see "When to use this" below).

## When to use this

Roofing is a trust-sensitive, YMYL-adjacent topic (see E-E-A-T requirements in the seo-writer skill). Humor should never undercut credibility or a proof point. Good candidates: light asides in intros/closers, blog posts about homeowner pain points (storm damage stress, contractor horror stories), listicles. Bad candidates: anything touching safety, pricing/warranty claims, insurance, or emergency/storm-damage service pages — keep those straight.

## Ground rules

**Do:**
- Be authentic — use humor that fits the brand voice, don't force a style that isn't genuine
- Self-deprecation over punching down
- A light touch — a little goes a long way, sprinkle rather than saturate
- Keep it PG-13 and universally relatable, not hyper-specific or inside-baseball

**Don't:**
- Sarcasm, putdowns, or GROSS humor (gender-bashing, racist, obscene, sexual, swearing)
- Humor aimed at competitors, other people, or disadvantages
- Forced jokes, or borrowed jokes from other writers
- Controversial or polarizing references that could alienate part of the audience

## Seven techniques

1. **Aside ("psst")** — a short parenthetical or em-dash interjection that shares a "secret" with the reader: a self-aware comment, exaggeration, or rhetorical question. Builds intimacy with the reader.
   > "...a page about it on your Auntie Jean's personal blog (which is a shame because Auntie Jean is one hell of a twerker)."

2. **Alliteration** — a run of words sharing the same starting *sound* (not necessarily letter). Adds a lyrical, funnier cadence, especially in names and lists.
   > "Bert ogled the bevy of beautiful babes on the beach."

3. **Witty word choice** — swap flat words for punchier synonyms (plosive consonants — p, b, d, g, t, k — read as funnier), and swap vague nouns for specific, concrete ones.
   > "brouhaha" instead of "chaos"; "2003 Ford Focus had loose lug nuts" instead of "car needed a repair."

4. **Rule of Three / surprise ending** — two straight setup items, then a third that breaks the pattern with an absurd twist. Works in lists, bios, taglines, and sentence sequences.
   > "Paychecks stopped being deposited. Savings accounts started dwindling. Ramen noodle consumption skyrocketed."

5. **Exaggeration** — stretch a comparison to an absurd, extreme scale so the mismatch between reality and the image is obvious.
   > "...a valued brassiere of normal dimensions, but is now suitable only as a sun hat for a small, two-headed squirrel."

6. **Twisted cliché** — take a familiar saying and swap the expected ending for a surprising one.
   > "If at first you don't succeed, then skydiving isn't for you." (twist on "try, try again")

7. **Callback** — repeat or reference an earlier joke/line later in the piece, creating a payoff via familiarity. Effective as a closing device.

## Practical notes for drafts

- Confine humor to intros, closers, or transitional asides — never inside proof points, stats, or safety/pricing claims (breaks E-E-A-T trust signals).
- One or two techniques per post is plenty; don't stack all seven.
- If a joke risks reading as dated, regional, or exclusionary, cut it — universal beats clever.
- When in doubt, leave it out; a flat but trustworthy paragraph beats a joke that lands wrong on a roofing YMYL page.
